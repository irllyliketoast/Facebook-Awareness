Cybersecurity Awareness: The Truth About Facebook Messenger

Imagine if every time you had a conversation in your home, someone was standing outside your window, writing down everything you said, noting who was in the room, and even keeping track of the furniture in your house. Sounds invasive, right? That’s essentially what Messenger does—but instead of a stranger outside, it’s a multi-billion-dollar company, Facebook. Facebook Messenger isn’t just an app for chatting—it’s one of the most invasive data collectors on your phone. While most apps gather some basic data, Messenger takes it to a whole new level. If you have it installed, it’s not just tracking you—it’s collecting details from every device on the network you’re connected to, including: your IP Address, Device Name, and MAC Address.

That means every time you connect to a Wi-Fi network, Messenger scans and logs every device on it. Your friend’s phone? Logged. Your laptop? Logged. Even smart TVs and home assistants get recorded. And who owns this data? A private American company with no obligation to protect your privacy.

But this isn’t new—Facebook was built on stolen data. It started as “Facemash,” a Harvard project where Mark Zuckerberg hacked school servers to steal student photos and create a website rating women’s attractiveness. That obsession with data theft never stopped. Facebook didn’t innovate social media—it stole the idea from early networking sites like MySpace and Friendster. And when it couldn’t buy out rising competitors like Snapchat or TikTok, it cloned their features instead.

The bigger problem is that there are no formal ethical laws in cybersecurity. Companies like Facebook don’t have a legal obligation to protect user data—they do the bare minimum, often after getting caught mishandling it. That’s how breaches like the Ashley Madison leak happened. The site, known for facilitating extramarital affairs, stored highly sensitive user data without proper encryption. When hackers exposed it, millions of users had their personal lives destroyed, leading to blackmail, divorces, and even some to take their own life. Or take DeepSeek, where AI-generated deepfake leaks threaten privacy on a scale never seen before. The lack of clear cybersecurity regulations means we’re constantly playing defense, waiting for the next major breach instead of preventing it. That’s why privacy isn’t just a software issue—it’s a personal responsibility. We have to take cybersecurity seriously, not just when developing software, but also in our own homes. That means limiting the data we share, questioning what apps we install, and understanding that if something is free—we are the product.

If you’re using Messenger, you’re feeding one of the most aggressive data-harvesting machines in history. Maybe it’s time to reconsider how much access we’re giving to a company that built its empire on stolen information.
